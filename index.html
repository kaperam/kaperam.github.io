Towards the understanding of the formation and evolution of low mass stars, brown dwarfs and planets in star forming regions.

Since about two decades ago, the discovery of a numerous population of substellar objects, especially isolated planetary mass objects (with masses below the deuterium burning–mass limit at ∼13 MJup) challenges our understanding of the formation processes that governs the birth and evolution of such low mass objects. The proper characterization of young planetary–mass objects and the analysis of their membership in known star forming regions are necessary steps for an accurate derivation of the substellar mass function. Young and well characterized star forming regions offers an excellent ground for the exploration of a young planetary mass population and the definition of the substellar mass function. The σ Orionis cluster (∼3Myr, ∼352pc, low extinction, solar metallicity), harbors the largest clustered isolated planetary mass population known to date in our Galaxy. Here we propose to conduct an unprecedented study in this star forming region pointing at refining its substellar mass function, identifing if there is a low–mass end for the formation of objects in isolation and to study the kinematics properties of the different mass populations in the cluster. By the spectroscopic confirmation of the sources at the planetary masses in the σ Orionis cluster we will be able to remove contaminants and identify bona-fide planetary cluster members. To complete the spectroscopic characterization of the mass interval 6–12MJup in σ Orionis, would lead to the determination of the most reliable and unambiguously derived substellar mass function ever known. In addition, by perfoming an astrometric study at planetary masses we will be able to address if there are kinematic differences beyond the error bars between the distinct populations in the σ Orionis cluster. The study of the mass function and the kinematics of the lowest masses in stellar clusters and star forming regions can help us recognizing the formation mechanism of the isolated planetary mass objects. In particular, one of the major goals of stellar and substellar astrophysics is to know whether this process is common in nature and whether there is a minimum mass for the formation of these objects. With an exploration of the σ Orionis cluster area reaching deeper magnitudes in the Z, J and H bands it would be possible to identify the opacity fragmentation limit for the first time in an entire star forming region. In addition, because of the young age of the cluster, the results will become a reference to understand young objects in the field and gas–giant planets.
